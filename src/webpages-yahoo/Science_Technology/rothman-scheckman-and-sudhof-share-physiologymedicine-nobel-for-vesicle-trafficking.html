    Rothman, Scheckman, And Südhof Share Physiology/Medicine Nobel For Vesicle Trafficking

    comments, called-out
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments
    Comment Now
    Follow Comments Following Comments Unfollow Comments

    Examples for subcellular localisations (Photo credit: Wikipedia)

    I grew up during the heyday of molecular biology. How genes gave rise to specific proteins. How genes were regulated. How genes might one day be transferred from humans to other organisms to make factory-like quantities.

    What wasn’t stressed in my early education was exactly how those proteins that were made by cells were actually released – to do something. Insulin can’t work unless it gets outside of the beta cells of the pancreas. Modern antidepressants only work because they prevent neurons from reuptake of the neurotransmitter serotonin. But how was that serotonin released in the first place?

    What some might call the less glamorous work of cell biology is certainly the more complicated and difficult. So today, you’ll be hearing a lot of discussion about cellular structures called vesicles. These bubble-like bags of cellular goodies are one common way that the cells gets things from the inside to the outside, sending off their chemical messages from a few micrometers away to a couple of meters.

    From the Nobel Prize announcement a few moments ago:

    Randy Schekman discovered a set of genes that were required for vesicle traffic. James Rothman  unravelled protein machinery that allows vesicles to fuse with their targets to permit transfer of cargo. Thomas Südhof revealed how signals instruct vesicles to release their cargo with precision.

    Through their discoveries, Rothman, Schekman and Südhof have revealed the exquisitely precise control system for the transport and delivery of cellular cargo. Disturbances in this system have deleterious effects and contribute to conditions such as neurological diseases, diabetes, and immunological disorders.

     

    Thomson Reuters was rather off with their predictions this year for the medicine prize (by the way, the proper terminology for the award is The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. Somewhat cumbersome but exactly as listed in Alfred Nobel’s will.) The parent company of the unit that establishes measure of journal impact by quantifying the number a frequency of citations of one’s work by other scientists, Thomson Reuters began announcing their Thomson Reuters Citation Laureates in 2002, generally using their metrics for predicting prize winners. This year was a miss as they predicted leaders in a) the area of gene methylation as a means of gene regulation, b) the process by which cells die and consume themselves (autophagy), or c) the discovery and medical exploitation of the HER2/neu protein.

    Somewhat ironic is that the cell membrane location of the HER2/neu receptor and autophagy, to a lesser extent, makes use of the principles and processes elucidated by the work of today’s new Nobel laureates.

    Of particular note is that Randy Scheckman, who has been at the University of California at Berkeley for 35 years, did most of his work using yeast. Once again, so-called model systems using non-human cells or organisms has given us crucial insights to how our bodies function in health and disease.

    One additional observation: Two of the three laureates trained themselves with previous Nobel laureates. Scheckman did his PhD work in 1974 with legendary molecular biologist Arthur Kornberg (Physiology or Medicine 1959) while Südhof was a postdoctoral fellow with the cholesterol biology pioneers at the University of Texas Southwestern Medical School, Michael Brown and Joseph Goldstein (Physiology or Medicine 1985).
