When Problems Arise: Machine or an Anesthesiologist?

Sedasys: The reality is that no machine can replace the skills of a physician.

  • Article
  • Stock Quotes
  • Comments
more in Opinion | Find New $LINKTEXTFIND$ »
  • smaller
  • Larger

While Johnson & Johnson's new Sedasys machine is able to administer sedation in very limited circumstances ("Robots vs. Anesthesiologists," Marketplace, Sept. 26), the reality is that no machine can replace the skills of a physician. Emergencies can and do occur during colonoscopies or any surgical procedure, even with the healthiest of patients. Patients are at risk unless there is a physician present, properly trained in rescue from unintended deep sedation and prepared and capable of addressing deterioration in the patient's life functions. In addition, the notion of cost savings may quickly be lost as additional physician support is necessary in the rescue of a patient from a life-threatening situation.

Our primary goal is to deliver the safest medical care. We will be watching closely because all patients deserve the vigilance of a trained physician.

John M. Zerwas, M.D.

Jane C.K. Fitch, M.D.

American Society of Anesthesiologists

Park Ridge, Ill.

I started doing colonoscopies and other procedures in my general surgical residency in 1974 and continued performing them until my retirement almost eight years ago. Over 95% of them were done without an anesthesiologist, and in all those years I had one respiratory arrest, which was handled quickly without any untoward effects.

We physicians need to start drawing a line on costs or the government will increasingly tell us what and how to do things. I applaud Johnson & Johnson for the Sedasys system and hope that the anesthesiology profession realizes that their protestations are very much like those of teachers unions.

Edmund I. Leff, M.D.

Paradise Valley, Ariz.

The Food and Drug Administration expects anesthesiologists to be "immediately available" should a crisis arrive with the Sedasys system. Just so we are clear then: As an anesthesiologist, I'm to be available to step in during an emergency, assume all liability for a patient I didn't screen, don't know and who is most likely incapable of rendering informed consent due to the presence of mind-altering medication. All for no compensation? Sign me up.

Greg Enders, M.D.

Seneca, S.C.

Beyond the uninformed excited enthusiasm of our non-M.D. administrators, I envision a very limited safe use for Sedasys.

Andrea Torri, M.D.

Harvard Medical School

Boston

As much science as there is, anesthesia care still is an art. If you want to have a robot manage you during your colonoscopy, have at it, but make sure your living will is in order so your health-care power of attorney knows your wishes in your persistent vegetative state.

Thomas W. Andrews, M.D.

Maitland, Fla.

A version of this article appeared October 6, 2013, on page A16 in the U.S. edition of The Wall Street Journal, with the headline: When Problems Arise: Machine or an Anesthesiologist?.
